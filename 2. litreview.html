2. Literature Review

2.1. Current PDTE Models

Tumor models must maintain tissue structure and stromal cell distribution for effective evaluation of stromal-targeting therapies [5]. PDTE preserves native architecture, cell-cell and cell-ECM interactions, and immune composition, making them essential for rapid drug testing in ex vivo studies [12].

Several culturing methods have been explored to enhance the viability of tumor explants.

2.1.1. Gelatin Sponge-based Cultures

PDE models using gelatin sponges are promising in preclinical drug testing and studying tumor-microenvironment interactions. Yet, these models often experience rapid tissue degradation and reduced viability within six days. This challenge was partially addressed by advanced slicing technologies like vibratomes and compresstomes, which ensure uniform sample thickness [5].

2.1.2. Matrigel

Matrigel, an extract of a mouse Engelbreth-Holm-Swarm (EHS) tumor, has facilitated the structural and functional characterization of its components by providing large amounts of ECM. It has been used for a variety of studies with embryonic, normal, and stem or malignant cells [16]. However, it only supports a short-term culture of 3 days, and the viability of PDE rapidly decreased afterwards [17].

2.1.3. Hyaluronan (HA)-based Scaffold

The Patient-derived hydrogel-assisted eXplants (PhaseX) hydrogel from the Translational Tumor Engineering (TTE) lab provides enhanced support for PDE compared to traditional PTFE membranes and Matrigel, using bioengineered hyaluronan (HA)-based hydrogels. HA’s similarity to extracellular matrix (ECM) makes it a promising scaffold for tissue culture [18]. PhaseX’s modifiable properties, such as stiffness, degradability, and adhesivity, preserve tissue integrity, cellular diversity, and the viability of cancer-associated fibroblasts (CAFs) and immune cells. However, adjustments for PDE from metastatic tumors may require lower matrix adhesivity to prevent excessive cell invasion, and highly vascularized tumors demand increased porosity for better nutrient and oxygen diffusion. Although effective, PhaseX’s HA hydrogel system cannot independently modulate HA concentration and stiffness, limiting its flexibility for varied applications.

2.2. Effects of Matrix Properties on PDTE

To establish an effective hydrogel-based model, it is crucial to understand how specific matrix properties interact with PDTE and influence their structural and functional stability. Key biophysical factors include architecture (e.g., dimensionality, topography, porosity) and mechanics (e.g., stiffness, elasticity), which have been shown to influence cancer cell behavior, particularly in proliferation and migration [13]. In this review, we examine the role of matrix properties in PDTE preservation, focusing on how these factors influence the maintenance of tumor-like behavior ex vivo. By integrating insights from recent studies, this review will provide a foundation for designing an optimized hydrogel capable of extending PDTE viability for precision oncology applications.

2.2.1. Stiffness

Matrix stiffness, the extracellular environment’s rigidity, affects cell behavior, stability and scaffold integrity [14 ]. Generally, stiffer matrices promote greater cell spreading, proliferation, and differentiation, as cells detect and respond to mechanical signals in their surroundings[15 ][1 6]. For example, hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) cells exhibit a rounded shape on softer matrices but spread extensively on stiffer ones [1 7]. Such spreading enhances nutrient absorption, which supports cell proliferation [17]. Studies on breast cancer cells cultured on agarose hydrogels of varying stiffness revealed that cells in stiffer hydrogels had higher viability under doxorubicin (DOX) treatment, suggesting that stiffer hydrogels may better mimic the increased stiffness of tumor tissue, thus improving chemoresistance study outcomes[18 ][19 ]. With breast cancer tissue stiffness typically over 4 kPa, stiffer hydrogel models may better simulate the tumor microenvironment and increase predictive accuracy for in vivo behavior [5].

PhaseX, a tunable HA-based hydrogel, demonstrated the impact of stiffness in ex vivo models. By adjusting HA to achieve 380 Pa stiffness, PhaseX maintained head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) explants for up to 9 days, preserving both tissue area and live cell count [18]. The hydrogel stiffness can also be adjusted by crosslinking HA with methacrylate anhydride to form methacrylated hyaluronic acid (MeHA/HAMA). Lu et al. showed that by adjusting the concentration of MA and the crosslinking and the ultraviolet light exposure, HAMA of different stiffness can be formed. Different stiffness of HAMA also resulted in different viability [lu]. Tailoring hydrogel stiffness to approximate the native tumor environment may have more physiologically relevant models, potentially increasing the predictability of ex vivo results for in vivo outcomes.

Interestingly, cancer cells migrating from spheroids can stiffen surrounding collagen matrices through strain-induced stiffening, showing that cell migration and hydrogel stiffness mutually influence one another in tumor modeling [20]. This bidirectional relationship highlights the importance of optimizing hydrogel stiffness to mimic real tumor conditions effectively.

2.2.2. Porosity

Even though higher stiffness tends to improve the viability of solid tumors, cells were observed to migrate from the dense collagen matrix to the loose collagen matrix, indicating that the cells showed a tendency toward a more compliant 3D matrix of collagen [19]. However, Sapudom et al. suggest that cell migration may be influenced by matrix porosity instead of stiffness, with cells migrating from small to larger pores [21 ]. Most bulk hydrogels have dense polymer networks with nano-sized pores, which are typically insufficient for nutrient diffusion, cell migration, and proliferation [22 ]. The microarchitecture, consists of pore size and fiber diameter, is shown to guide cellular behaviors such as myofibroblast differentiation independently of matrix stiffness [20][23].

Micropores can be formed by fabricating microspheres with gelatin. Gelatin is used as it is highly water soluble. It is a good porogen as pores can be formed fast and facilitate cell migration and proliferation. Lu et al. fabricated porous HAMA using gelatin microspheres (GMS) template method. It was found that human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs) migrated more significantly in macroporous HAMA than in non-macroporous ones, which restricted cell movement and concentrated cells on the hydrogel surface [23].

Pore structure is also crucial for vascularization. Porous HAMA supports greater vessel density and cell proliferation than its non-macroporous counterpart, with a pore diameter range of 200 to 250 µm being optimal for cell viability, migration, and vascularization compared to smaller or larger pores. This range is suitable for tumor cells which has an average diameter of 10 to 20 µm. This pore diameter can act as a guideline for designing hydrogel models [24].

2.2.3. Degradation

Hydrogel scaffold degradability is essential for providing temporary support that adapts as new tissue forms. Scaffold degradation can occur through enzymatic hydrolysis, ester hydrolysis, or photolytic cleavage of polymer chains, allowing the structure to break down gradually [25 ]. Precise control of degradation is vital to ensure the scaffold lasts long enough to support cells until the newly formed tissue can sustain itself. This degradation mimics dynamic physical cues of the extracellular matrix (ECM), influencing cell behavior critical for processes like spreading, migration, proliferation, and differentiation [26].

A common approach to tune hydrogel degradation is to incorporate matrix metalloproteinase (MMP)-sensitive crosslinkers. For example, methacrylated hyaluronic acid (MeHA) hydrogels with MMP-sensitive links allow human mesenchymal stem cells (hMSCs) to reshape their environment by secreting enzymes to degrade the matrix, which enhances cell spreading and differentiation. Studies show that hMSCs in MMP-sensitive hydrogels express higher chondrogenic markers and reduced hypertrophic markers, promoting cartilage formation while limiting calcification [27 ]. Similarly, HA hydrogels conjugated with MMP-sensitive peptides better preserved live cells and tissue morphology in patient-derived tumor slices (PDTS) than non-degradable gels, highlighting the importance of MMP-degradability in maintaining PDTS viability [18].

Thus, designing degradable hydrogels with controlled, cell-responsive degradation supports tissue development by providing crucial structural and biochemical cues.

2.2.4. Crosslinking Density

Crosslinking density affects stiffness, porosity, and degradability in hydrogels. Higher density increases stiffness and stability but reduces porosity, limiting nutrient diffusion and cell migration. This dense network also slows degradation [26]. Adjusting crosslinking balances these factors, enabling hydrogels to mimic tissue properties effectively, especially for accurate tumor modeling.
