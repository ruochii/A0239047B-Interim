2. Literature Review

2.1. Current PDTE Models

Tumor models must maintain tissue structure and stromal cell distribution for effective evaluation of stromal-targeting therapies [5]. PDTE preserves native architecture, cell-cell and cell-extracellular microenvironment (ECM) interactions, and immune composition, making them essential for rapid drug testing in ex vivo studies [12].

2.1.1. Gelatin Sponge-based Cultures

PDE models using gelatin sponges support preclinical drug testing and TME studies, but often experience tissue degradation and reduced viability within six days. This challenge was partially addressed by advanced slicing technologies like vibratomes to improve sample consistency [5].

2.1.2. Matrigel

A mouse tumor extract rich in ECM components, Matrigel is widely used for culturing embryonic and malignant cells, but it only supports a short-term culture of 3 days due to declining viability afterwards [13][14].

2.1.3. HA-based Scaffold

Developed by the Translational Tumor Engineering (TTE) lab, this HA-based hydrogel provides enhanced support for PDTE compared to traditional PTFE membranes and Matrigel. Its HA-based composition resembles native ECM, enhances tissue preservation by providing tunable properties like stiffness, degradability and adhesivity, thereby supporting cancer-associated fibroblasts and immune cells [14][15]. However, PhaseX cannot independently modulate HA concentration and stiffness, limiting its flexibility for varied applications [14].

2.2. Effects of Matrix Properties on PDTE

To establish an effective hydrogel-based model, it is crucial to understand how specific matrix properties influence the structural and functional stability. Key biophysical factors include architecture (e.g., dimensionality, topography, porosity) and mechanics (e.g., stiffness, elasticity), which have been shown to influence cancer cell behavior, particularly in proliferation and migration [16].

2.2.1. Stiffness and Cancer Cell Viability

Matrix stiffness affects the cell behavior, stability and scaffold integrity [17]. Generally, stiffer matrices promote greater cell spreading, proliferation, and differentiation[18][19]. For example, hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC) changed from a rounded shape on softer matrices to more spread out on stiffer ones, enhancing nutrient absorption, which supports cell proliferation [20]. Studies on breast cancer cells further demonstrate that stiffer hydrogels increase cell viability, even under chemotherapy (doxorubicin treatment), suggesting that stiffer hydrogels may better mimic the increased stiffness of tumor tissue, thus improving chemoresistance study outcomes [21][22].

PhaseX hydrogel with tunable stiffness maintained both the tissue area and live cell count of head and neck squamous cell carcinoma (HNSCC) explants for up to 9 days [14]. Crosslinking HA with methacrylate anhydride (MA) can form methacrylated hyaluronic acid (MeHA/HAMA), enabling stiffness adjustment to simulate the TME stiffness and increase the physiological relevance of ex vivo models, increasing the predictability of ex vivo results for in vivo outcomes [15].

Interestingly, cancer cells migrating from spheroids can stiffen surrounding collagen matrices through strain-induced stiffening, showing that cell migration and hydrogel stiffness mutually influence one another in tumor modeling [23]. This bidirectional relationship is essential for optimizing hydrogel stiffness to mimic real tumor conditions effectively.

2.2.2. Porosity and Nutrient Diffusion

Even though higher stiffness supports the viability of solid tumors, porosity enhances nutrient diffusion and cell migration [23]. Cells often migrate from dense to loose matrices, seeking compliant environments [22][24]. Most bulk hydrogels have dense nano-sized pores that limit nutrient diffusion, cell migration and proliferation [25].

Micropores formed by gelatin microspheres (GMS) templating, as used by Lu et al., can facilitate enhanced cell migration and vascularization. Gelatin is often used to form microspheres as it is a good porogen with high water solubility, pores can be formed fast and facilitate cell migration and proliferation. Lu et al. found that human umbilical vein endothelial cells (HUVECs) migrated more significantly in macroporous HAMA than in non-macroporous ones, which restricted cell movement and concentrated cells on the hydrogel surface [15][26]. Porous HAMA with a pore diameter range of 200µm to 250µm also supported higher cell viability, migration, and vascularization compared to smaller or larger pores as they align better with the typical tumor cell diameter of 10µm to 20µm [27].

2.2.3. Degradability and Scaffold Adaptability

Hydrogel scaffold degradability enables dynamic adaptation to cellular remodeling needs, mimicking ECM's natural turnover [28][29]. Incorporating metalloproteinase (MMP)-sensitive crosslinkers with MeHA hydrogels allow human mesenchymal stem cells (hMSCs) to reshape their environment by secreting enzymes to degrade the matrix, promoting cell spreading and differentiation. Studies show MMP-sensitive hydrogels better preserve tissue integrity and cell viability in PDTE compared to non-degradable hydrogels [14][30]. Thus, designing degradable hydrogels with controlled, cell-responsive degradation supports tissue development by providing crucial structural and biochemical cues.
