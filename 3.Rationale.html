3. Rationale

While current studies offer insights into stiffness and porosity as individual properties, they often examine them in isolation, overlooking their interdependent effects on tumor progression. Stiffness influences cancer cell behavior, mechanotransduction, and drug response, while porosity governs cell migration, nutrient diffusion, and overall scaffold integrity. Porosity significantly influences scaffold stiffness, as increased porosity generally leads to a decrease in mechanical strength [27]. Given their intertwined nature, it remains unclear how these factors independently and collectively shape the TME in PDTE models. This gap highlights the need to design hydrogels with independently tunable stiffness and porosity to systematically dissect their roles. By doing so, we can optimize PDTE cultures, creating a more physiologically relevant ex vivo model that enhances drug testing and expands applications in precision oncology.

3.1. Objectives and Significance of Study

This study aims to establish a protocol for a separate and precise control of hydrogel stiffness and porosity within the hydrogel system, to extend the PDTE viability and functions for drug testings in the future. Ultimately, we aim to determine the parameter which plays the major role in long term PDTE culture.

By optimizing the hydrogel’s stiffness and porosity, this study will:

  1. Enhance model fidelity as hydrogel scaffold with properties matching tumour tissues can improve the preservation of tissue integrity, viability, and cellular interactions in PDTE.
  2. Provide insight into the implication of stiffness, porosity, and cell adhesiveness in PDTE culture will guide future PDTE model development, supporting advances in cancer research.
  3. Enable precise and clinically relevant drug testing (e.g., cyclic drug testing), a cornerstone for improved treatment compliance and patient’s well-being.

3.2. Hypotheses

We hypothesize that matching hydrogel stiffness to PDTE-specific mechanical properties will enhance viability, mechanotransduction signalling and drug response in ex vivo culture.

  1. Methacrylation of HA enables tunable crosslinking, allowing precise control over stiffness, which is essential for supporting different tumor microenvironments.
  2. Hydrogel stiffness is directly proportional to the amount of methacrylation and photoinitiator LAP.
  3. Small pore sizes may restrict cell migration and limit nutrient diffusion, while excessively large pores may reduce available surface area for cell adhesion, yet supporting nutrient diffusion.
  4. Incorporating a templating approach with the optimal pore size will improve nutrient diffusion and cell migration, promoting sustained PDTE survival.

As such, we propose MehAir, a MeHA-based hydrogel system with an air-liquid interface and rotational unit designed to enhance gas and nutrient exchange, mimicking the tumor extracellular matrix for improved PDTE viability (Figure 6). This study focuses on developing the protocol for tuning stiffness and porosity individually.
