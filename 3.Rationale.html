3. Rationale

While current studies offer insights into individual properties mentioned above, they often do so in isolation. Furthermore, many studies focus on cell structures or isolated cellular behaviors, which may not fully replicate the complex TME. Therefore, there is limited understanding of how these factors interact to support the TME in PDTE models. This gap suggests that hydrogel scaffolds combining tunable stiffness, porosity and degradability could better simulate the in vivo TME, improving the model’s application for drug testing. This study will address this need by systematically investigating how these combined hydrogel properties influence PDTE viability, structural integrity and cellular behavior over extended periods, offering a more comprehensive approach to PDTE preservation for precision oncology. This research is divided into two main components: material fabrication and biological analysis.

3.1 Hypotheses

For material fabrication, we hypothesize:

  1. Increasing the concentration of MA will increase the hydrogel stiffness by increasing crosslinking density.
  2. Using gelatin microsphere templating methods will control pore size, where larger microspheres result in greater porosity, facilitating nutrient diffusion.
  3. Incorporating MMP-sensitive crosslinkers will enable controlled degradation, allowing the scaffold to break down gradually in response to cellular activity.
  4. Variations in stiffness, porosity and degradability will influence each other, thereby affecting the overall behavior of tumor tissues in the hydrogel matrix. We hypothesize that balancing these parameters will maximize PDTE preservation.

For biological analysis, we hypothesize:

  1. Tuning stiffness of hydrogel to similar to that of tumor tissue will enhance the viability and structural integrity of PDTE by more accurately replicating in vivo mechanical conditions.
  2. Higher porosity in hydrogel scaffold will improve nutrient and oxygen diffusion, leading to increased cell viability and reduced apoptosis in PDTE.
  3. Controlled hydrogel degradation via MMP-sensitive crosslinkers will support cell-mediated remodeling, improve cell survival and gene expression profiles closer to those observed in vivo.
  4. The combination of optimized stiffness, porosity and degradability will yield improved viability and structural integrity in PDTE, offering the best conditions for ex vivo culture and drug testing.

3.2. Objectives and Experimental Strategies

This section outlines the systematic approach to develop a tunable MeHA-based platform for PDTE culture, focusing on establishing a protocol that allows independent control of stiffness, porosity and degradation, such that the platform can optimize PDTE preservation and enable effective ex vivo analysis.

3.2.1. Aim 1: Hydrogel Fabrication and Characterization

Objective: Develop a tunable MeHA-based hydrogel scaffold with adjustable stiffness, porosity, and degradability for PDTE support.

Methods:

  1. Modify MA concentration for stiffness.
  2. Fabricate and use GMS to control porosity.
  3. Incorporate MMP-sensitive crosslinker (PQ) for degradability.
  4. Characterize hydrogel properties with rheology and microscopy to confirm target specifications.

3.2.2. Aim 2: Biological Analysis of PDTE Response to Hydrogel Properties

Objective: Assess PDTE viability, structural integrity, proliferation, and apoptosis in response to hydrogel properties.

Methods:

  1. Use confocal microscopy and histology for structural analysis.
  2. Multiplex immunohistochemistry (mIHC) to quantify cell proliferation and tissue remodeling.
  3. Caspase-3 staining for apoptosis detection.
  4. RNA sequencing to examine gene expression changes associated with survival and apoptosis markers.

3.2.3. Aim 3: Optimization of Hydrogel Parameters for Enhanced PDTE Preservation

Objective: Correlate hydrogel parameters with PDTE outcomes to determine optimal scaffold conditions for long-term culture.

Methods:

  1. Analyze PDTE viability, apoptosis, proliferation, and gene expression under various hydrogel properties.
  2. Iteratively refining the scaffold to optimize tissue preservation.

3.3. Significance of the Study

By optimizing the hydrogel’s stiffness, porosity, and degradability, this research will:

  1. Enhance model fidelity as tunable hydrogel scaffold will better replicate TME, preserving tissue integrity, viability, and cellular interactions in PDTE.
  2. Provide insight into TME requirements as analysis of stiffness, porosity, and degradability effects will guide future PDTE model development, supporting advances in cancer research.
  3. Support for drug testing and precision oncology as optimized scaffold can potentially enabling long-term drug testing and improving preclinical prediction accuracy with extended culture period.
