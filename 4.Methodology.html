4. Methodology

4.1. Tissue Processing

Tumors were collected from the National Cancer Center Singapore (NCCS). Samples were processed within two to five hours after resection. Before processing, tumor tissues were washed three times with sterile Hank's Balanced Salt Solution (HBSS) buffer. Then, prepared at a thickness of 300 µm using a precision-cut vibrating-blade microtome (Leica VT1200S). Following the slicing process, the slices were placed in a complete culture medium (1X DMEM/F12 media enriched with 10% fetal bovine serum, 1% Penicillin-Streptomycin, 0.1mg/ml Primocin, and 1X ROCK pathway inhibitor) and incubated under standard conditions (37 °C, 90% humidity, 13.5% CO2) for one hour to recover. After this recovery phase, the slices were randomly distributed to MeHA gels.

4.2. Porous MeHA Fabrication Process and Characterization

4.2.1.MeHA Synthesis

0.5g of HA was dissolved in 50 mL of mixed solvent of Dimethylformamide (DMF) and H2O (1:2 v/v), stirred at 700 rpm overnight in the 4 ℃ fridge. MA was added dropwise into the solution, adjusting the pH to 8.5 using 4 M NaOH. The reaction continued around 24 hours in the 4 ℃ fridge with stirring. Throughout the reaction, the pH was maintained at 8.5 with a second pH adjustment after the first 10 hours. Subsequently, MeHA was dialyzed for 5 days in a dialysis membrane with a molecular weight cutoff of 3.5 kDa after centrifugation (5000 rpm, 5 minutes) to remove precipitates. Finally, the MeHA was freeze-dried for 3 days and stored at 4 ℃ for future use.

4.2.2.GMP Synthesis

4.2.2.1. Protocol for GMP₁₄₀, GMP₃₀₀, GMP₄₄₀

500mg of HA was dissolved in 50ml of Dimethylformamide (DMF): water mixture with a 1:2 volume/volume ratio. The mixture was stirred continuously overnight at 4°C. The solution's pH was then adjusted to 9 using 4M sodium hydroxide (NaOH). MA was added drop by drop, ensuring the pH remained at 9 for the duration of the reaction, followed by a 5-day dialysis process using a Thermo Scientific SnakeSkin™ 3.5K MWCO, 16 mm I.D. Dialysis Tubing [31]. The MeHA was then freeze-dried and stored at 4°C until needed.

4.1.2. Fabrication of GMS

GMS were fabricated by emulsion. 30mL of a 10% gelatin solution was incorporated dropwise into 60mL of soybean oil with continuous stirring for 5 minutes. The water-oil emulsion mixture was transferred into a 4°C fridge to cool the mixture to 10°C with continuous stirring. The mixture was filtered with 2µm filter paper and thoroughly washed with acetone, followed by oven drying in 40°C oven overnight. The fabricated GMS were filtered with a set of standard sieves (mesh size: 150µm, 250µm, 315µm and 400µm) and divided into groups.

4.1.3. Fabrication of GMS Template

Polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) mold with 6mm diameter with 2mm height wells were filled with GMS of desired size and increased packing with slight taping. GMS was soaked with 40μL of 90% ethanol and annealed in 40°C oven to allow the particles to adhere to each other and form a well-packed porogen template.

4.1.4. Fabrication of Porous MeHA Gel

To prepare the precursor solution, 1.5%MeHA and 0.5% photoinitiator LAP were dissolved in water. Subsequently, the GMS template was immersed in the solution under vacuum. The resultant mixture was then exposed to ultraviolet light (405 m, 30 mW cm−2) for 2 minutes to induce crosslinking.

4.2. Materials Characterization

4.2.1. Stiffness Control

We synthesized MeHA hydrogels with 10 distinct stiffness levels by modulating the concentration of LAP in the hydrogel solution. The stiffness of these hydrogels is directly proportional to LAP concentration, ranging from 97Pa to 2.4kPa (Figure 16).

4.2.2. Pore Structure Generation

GMS were fabricated as shown in Figure 13. Fine particles were expected after the washing and drying stage, but aggregation persisted even after vigorous shaking during washing. To address this, clumps due to aggregation were cut into smaller pieces with scissors. This led to irregular particles forming and caused the formation of irregular pore shapes. This deviated from our goal of creating a uniform pore structure that would serve as a more ideal physical microenvironment for controlled proliferation.

It was found that more clumps were formed when oil was fully removed from the residue during the filtration step. Therefore, we attempted to collect the residue when the residue was still moist and less clumps observed.

Using the refined filtration step, we successfully created three distinct GMS size range with round particles, resulting in hydrogels with uniform pore diameters in three categories: less than 100μm, 100μm to 200μm, and 200μm to 300μm. This uniformity in pore size will enhance the consistency of the microenvironment within the hydrogel, supporting more controlled cellular interactions and nutrient diffusion.

Since the average size of a tumor cell is 10 to 20 microns, we also aim to fabricate pores smaller than 10μm to investigate cell behavior and interactions within a confined space [27]. To achieve this, we will first assess the smallest GMS size range we can obtain from the less than 100μm batch using smaller sieves. If the resulting GMS size range is not below 10μm, we will fabricate gelatin nanospheres by adjusting the parameters in our existing protocol.
