5. Results and Discusssion

5.1. Stiffness Fabrication and Characterization

As a preliminary study, the team had previously developed five MeHA formula with a wide range of stiffness levels, and tested the crosslinking time of the MeHA (Appendix A).

5.1.1. Tissue Stiffness Test

To better replicate the stiffness of various tumor tissues, we first characterized the stiffness distribution of various tissue types by measuring Young’s modulus with atomic force microscope (AFM). As shown in Figure 12a, the tissue stiffness varied significantly across different groups, with mean Young’s modulus values ranging from 1.1 kPa (LM#2) to 6.8 kPa (OC#4). Most samples exhibited stiffness values between 0.1 to 3 kPa, whereas stiffer tissues (e.g., CRC#6, OC#6) displayed Young’s modulus values extending beyond 4 kPa. These variations highlight the heterogeneity in tumor microenvironments. Therefore, there is a need for biomaterials that can mimic a broad range of mechanical properties.

5.1.2. Development of Tunable Stiffness

To recapitulate these diverse mechanical environments, we further expanded the stiffness range by varying the LAP concentrations, in addition to the concentrations of MeHA. The Young's modulus of the resulting hydrogels is proportional to the LAP concentration, ranging from 0.29 kPa (MeHA-1, 1% LAP) to 7.14 kPa (MeHA-5, 1% LAP), covering a wide spectrum of stiffness level (Figure 12b). This tunability is essential for creating ex vivo models that better represent PDTE under physiologically relevant conditions.

5.1.3. Impact of Hydrogel Stiffness on Cell Viability

The effect of hydrogel stiffness on cell viability was assessed by measuring normalized cell viability over a 12-day culture period (Figure 12c). HA-5 promoted the highest cell viability over time. Therefore, this formula was used for further studies of the other parameters. It was observed that high stiffness hydrogels (HA-7 to HA10) exhibited a decline in viability. These findings align with previous studies demonstrating that excessive matrix stiffness can impair cell proliferation and viability, likely due to increased cellular stress and reduced nutrient diffusion. Therefore, to improve nutrient diffusion, particularly for long-term culture systems, GMP plates were included to enhance porosity for cell growth.

5.2. GMP Fabrication and Pore Characterization

To create pores for cell migration and nutrient diffusion, GMP of different sizes were added to MeHA (Section 3.2.2.1). We hypothesized that smaller pores may confine cell movement, while the larger pores would allow multicellular aggregates and stromal interactions. Initially, clumps formed after filtration and even vortexing (Figure 13b) due to excessive filtration, removing stabilizing oil. A scissor-cutting step was done for particle dispersion but led to irregular GMP (Figure 13c) and poorly interconnected pores with poor shapes after GMP dissolution (Figure 13d). This is undesirable since pore interconnectivity and shape affect cell proliferation, while irregular or collapsed pores can limit diffusion, causing necrosis [38][39] .

5.2.1. Optimization of GMP and Pore Fabrication

To reduce aggregation, we moderated oil filtration, collecting GMP while still moist. The residual oil was removed by washing with excess acetone and vortex until the particles became white and the supernatant was clear. This reduced clump formation, avoiding the scissor cutting step. SEM analysis (Figure 13e) further confirmed that most GMP were individually dispersed and had a circular shape, with fewer aggregates even among the larger particles, resulting in more interconnected and circular pores (Figure 13g). They were analyzed with Image J and presented in Figure 13h and 13i. Every group was named after the average size calculated.

Three larger GMP size and the resultant pore sizes were successfully fabricated. Since tumor cells range from 10 to 20 µm, we fabricated a smaller GMP to create diverse pore sizes for studying cell behavior [40]. We attempted to lower the mass and increased the stirring speed using the GMP protocol as described in Section 4.2.2.1. The average particle size obtained was around 100 µm. As such, we explored a double emulsion (O/W/O) method. After trying 0.5 g, 1 g and 2 g gelatin with 1000 rpm stirring speed, we observed the following (Figure 13k).

At 0.5 g, 1000 rpm, the resulting particles were smaller than 50 µm but highly irregular in shape. Increasing the gelatin mass to 1 g, stirring at 1000 rpm, a well-defined, spherical morphology with a mean size of approximately 50 µm, suggesting an optimal balance between shear force and interfacial tension during emulsification. Interestingly, when we used the same condition but a 1000ml beaker (2000ml beaker for the rest) with the same volume of ethanol, a higher yield of particles was obtained but with excessive fragmentation, likely due to intensified shear stress in the smaller beaker. Further increasing the gelatin mass to 2 g, stirring at 1000 rpm led to significantly larger particles (100 µm) with some coalescence, likely due to the increased viscosity reducing droplet breakup efficiency. Based on these findings, the 1 g, 1000 rpm condition was selected for the smallest pore formation. Following the trend (Figure 13j), even though the average actual particle size was 60 µm, the resultant pore size was 30 µm, smaller than the particle size. This pore size could provide sufficient space for individual cells to migrate.

To improve efficiency, we fabricated a new mold for GMP plate production. Initially, the GMP plates were fabricated in a rectangular PDMS mold with 2 mm height, and the GMP plates were obtained using a punch biopsy of a 6 mm radius (Figure 13l). However, precise annealing was needed to prevent sticking or fracturing (Figure 13m). To simplify this, a PDMS mold with 6 mm wells (Figure 10) was used, allowing direct GMP formation. This eliminated punching, extended annealing time and ensured consistent, easily demolded plates without strict timing control. To conclude, four sizes of particles and resultant spatially defined porosity and crosslinking were fabricated. A size difference between the particles and pores formed was observed, with an escalation correlating with the increase in particle and pore sizes.

5.3. Stiffness Characterization of Porous MeHA

With the optimized porous MeHA protocol, stiffness characterization was conducted again with the inclusion of 140 µm GMP plates and 2mg/ml of RGD for all conditions. A new range of stiffness was established. It could be observed that compared to MeHA without GMP plates (Figure 12), the Young's modulus tested was slightly lower since pores were formed, resisting the gel’s ability to resist deformation. The first condition will continue to be used for further material studies.

5.4. GMP Dissolution Test

To determine the GMP plates dissolution rate, a dissolution test was conducted as described in Section 4.2.5..

All porous MeHA exhibited a progressive increase in dissolution, reaching near-complete dissolution (~100%) by 5th hour. MeHA_60 showed the slowest dissolution rate, followed by MeHA_140 and MeHA_290, indicating that small GMP with small gaps in between particles, were harder to penetrate, which may affect initial nutrient diffusion into the gel during culture. This results suggest that finding an optimal GMP, hence pore size for culture is crucial for balancing between controlling cell migration and nutrient diffusion. Additionally, fluctuations in dissolution percentages over time could be attributed to aggregation effects or slight mass difference in the GMP plates used. The error bars indicate variability across replicates, suggesting that while the trends are consistent, further optimization of dissolution conditions may enhance reproducibility.

5.5. Preliminary Culture

Although the MeHA hydrogel model was not fully optimised, a preliminary study assessed its suitability for tissue culture. HA-5 with 2mg/ml RGD and MeHA_140 were used to culture melanoma and ovarian cancer tumor explants. Medium was changed every two days from day 0. Surface area images were taken on days 0, 3, 7, and 12 for ImageJ analysis. Confocal microscopy on days 3, 7, and 12 assessed viabilities. Figure 16a and 16b show the initial sample conditions.

5.5.1. Cell Viability

Melanoma cell viability increased over time, with a significant difference between MeHA and PTFE at Day 12 (p < 0.05), suggesting MeHA supports sustained growth. In contrast, ovarian cancer cells showed consistently low viability in both conditions, potentially due to samples not properly submerged into after extraction. A buffer maintains the physiological conditions of the tissue during transportation, essential for cellular viability and function. Confocal images further supported these findings, showing higher melanoma cell density in MeHA, while ovarian cancer cells, exhibited limited spreading and clustering, reinforcing reduced viability observations at later time points (D7, D12).

5.5.2. Surface Area Change

For melanoma, explants maintained a stable surface area over 12 days, with no significant changes across time points or between materials, suggesting MeHA supports morphological preservation similarly to PTFE. Ovarian cancer samples expanded over time, particularly in MeHA, likely due to rehydration after inadequate buffer submersion post-resection. MeHA’s high water retention and the structural loosening due to ECM remodeling support might have contributed to this expansion. In contrast, PTFE, which is a more inert material with limited hydration potential, did not induce the same degree of swelling. These findings highlight the importance of standardized tissue handling in hydrogel-based models to ensure consistent and reliable measurements of tumor behavior.
